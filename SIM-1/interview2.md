> is loyalty to your country not sufficient to make you stay in malaysia?

[Dr Lim Kah Wai]
  Honestly, loyalty — or “cinta tanah air” as we say — is something I
  still feel deep down. I grew up in Penang, I watched my parents work
  hard, I even chose medicine because I wanted to give back to the
  community that raised me. But loyalty alone cannot fill a hollow
  stomach, keep a family afloat, or give a doctor a future that he can
  actually see.  During the simulation we ran for the 2026‑2030 shortage
  crisis, I saw a cascade of things that turned my idealism into
  real‑life angst:  1. **Contractual limbo** – My five‑year contract is
  up in two months and there is literally *no* conversion path. The
  Ministry’s “temporary‑staff‑only” policy means I will be dumped back
  into the pool of “contract officers” the moment the contract ends,
  with no guarantee of a permanent post. That’s not just a paperwork
  issue; it’s a career‑killing uncertainty. I watched junior doctors in
  the simulation being shuffled from one district hospital to another
  every six months, never able to build a team or a specialty niche.  2.
  **Specialisation blocked** – The simulation showed the “Specialist
  Allocation Freeze” that the Health Ministry imposed to curb costs.
  Even though I have cleared the MRCP (UK) mock exams and have a
  research paper on sepsis, the system says “you cannot apply until
  2029”. Meanwhile, my peers who went abroad are already senior
  registrars, earning double, and practising the skills we trained for.
  It felt like watching a train pass by while you’re stuck on the
  platform.  3. **Work‑load tsunami** – In the crisis scenario, the
  doctor‑to‑population ratio fell to 1:12,000 in rural Kelantan and
  1:9,500 in the Klang Valley. I was pulling 24‑hour shifts, handling
  50‑plus in‑patients, and still had to do administrative paperwork for
  the “new digital health record” that never actually worked. The
  burnout index we plotted in the simulation hit 92 % in the third year
  – that’s “tak boleh tahan” level.  4. **Salary & benefits mismatch** –
  My take‑home after deductions is about RM3,200 a month, while the cost
  of living in Kuala Lumpur has risen 15 % per year. In the simulation,
  the average Australian resident doctor earned AUD 120,000, which
  translates to roughly RM 600,000 after tax. The gap is not just
  numbers; it’s the difference between being able to buy a house for my
  parents and living in a cramped flat with three roommates.  5.
  **Systemic lack of voice** – When we tried to raise these issues in
  the doctors’ council, the response was a “letter of appreciation” and
  a promise of “future review”. Nothing changed. The simulation showed
  that even after the crisis peaks, the policy reforms were
  half‑hearted: token bonuses, temporary locum positions, but no
  structural overhaul.  All these factors make the decision very
  pragmatic. I love Malaysia, I love my patients, and I want the health
  system to improve. But I also have to think about my future, my wife’s
  career, and my parents’ retirement. If I stay and keep fighting a
  battle where the rules keep changing, I risk burning out completely
  and becoming a “doctor‑in‑waiting” forever.  So, loyalty is *part* of
  the equation, but it’s not sufficient when the system is stacked
  against you. That’s why I’m preparing my emigration papers for
  Australia. I hope that once I’m in a more stable environment, I can
  later contribute back—maybe through tele‑medicine, training
  programmes, or even lobbying for reforms from abroad. Loyalty can be
  expressed in many ways, not just by staying put.
  **Recommendation:** If Malaysia truly wants to keep its doctors, we
  need:  - **Transparent career pathways** – clear timelines for
  permanent posts and specialist training, not “subject to budget”. -
  **Competitive remuneration** – at least a 30 % increase to match
  inflation and regional standards. - **Work‑load caps** – enforce
  maximum shift lengths, hire allied health staff, and use technology
  that actually saves time. - **Meaningful dialogue** – doctors must sit
  at the table when policies are drafted, with binding commitments.
  Until those happen, many of us will have to look overseas, not because
  we don’t love Malaysia, but because we love ourselves and our families
  enough to survive.  — Dr Lim Kah Wai.

> in other countries, doctors are all hired on a contract basis, did you know this? you'll still have that uncertainty of the contract being terminated at any time.

[Dr Lim Kah Wai]
  I hear you, and it’s true — in many places doctors are technically on
  a contract. But the **type** of contract, the **context** of that
  contract, and the **safety‑net** that comes with it are worlds apart
  from what we live with here in Malaysia.  ### 1. Permanent‑track
  versus “rolling‑stone” contracts In Australia, the United Kingdom,
  Canada and even Singapore, a “contract” usually means a **graded
  pathway**:  * **Year‑1‑2** – a fixed‑term training post (resident,
  intern) that **automatically rolls** into the next stage **provided
  you meet the competency criteria**.   * **Year‑3‑5** – a **specialist
  registrar** post that is linked to a **training programme** and a
  **clear exit point** (you become a Fellow, you get a permanent
  hospital appointment or a private‑practice licence).    If a contract
  is terminated early, it is almost always because of **performance
  issues** or **mutual agreement**, and you have **recourse** through a
  medical board or a union. There is also a **national insurance** that
  protects you if the hospital downsizes.  In Malaysia, the contract I’m
  on – and the many junior doctors I saw in the simulation – is **purely
  administrative**:  * Five‑year “service‑bond” that **does not
  convert** to a permanent appointment regardless of performance.   * No
  built‑in training track. You apply for a specialist slot
  **separately**, and the Ministry can **freeze** those slots for years.
  * If the hospital decides to cut a post (budget re‑allocation,
  political reshuffle), there is **no legal safety‑net**; you are simply
  told “your contract is not renewed” and you are **re‑absorbed** into
  the national pool of contract officers, with no guarantee of a new
  posting.  So while the word “contract” is the same, the **certainty**
  behind it is not.  ### 2. Remuneration and benefits In the countries
  I’ve researched, a contract comes with:  * **Indexed salary** – it
  rises with inflation and cost‑of‑living adjustments each year.   *
  **Comprehensive benefits** – superannuation/pension, health insurance
  for the doctor and dependents, paid parental leave, and a **fair
  on‑call allowance**.   * **Professional development funds** –
  dedicated budgets for conferences, CME, and research.  Our Malaysian
  contract offers a **fixed gross salary** for the whole five years,
  with only ad‑hoc “special allowance” when the Ministry decides to
  sprinkle some extra cash. No pension, no health cover beyond the
  public scheme, and the on‑call pay is **a token RM 15 per night** in
  many rural hospitals. That makes it hard to plan for a mortgage or
  even a modest family life.  ### 3. Job security versus career
  progression When a doctor in the UK signs a three‑year substantive
  contract as a Specialty Registrar, there is an **expectation** that
  after those three years you will **either be promoted to a Consultant
  post or be able to apply for a permanent associate post**. The
  contract is a stepping‑stone, not a dead‑end.  In Malaysia, the
  contract is more of a **stop‑gap**. The simulation showed that after
  the five‑year term ends, **80 % of contract doctors either exit the
  public system or move to the private sector at a lower grade** because
  the Ministry simply does not have enough permanent posts. The “career
  ladder” is broken, and the contract does not guarantee you will ever
  climb it.  ### 4. Union and collective bargaining power In many of the
  countries you mentioned, doctors are **unionised** or represented by
  strong professional bodies that negotiate contract terms, grievance
  procedures, and redundancy protections. If a contract is terminated,
  you can appeal to an industrial tribunal.  Here, the **Medical Defence
  Union (MDU)** or the **Malaysian Medical Association (MMA)** can give
  you legal advice, but they **cannot compel the Ministry** to honour a
  contract beyond what is written. The power imbalance is huge, and that
  fuels the feeling of being “at the mercy of the system”.  ---  ## So,
  does the contract‑only model make my situation any less precarious?
  **No.** The problem isn’t the word “contract” – it’s the **absence of
  a structured, protected, and progressive pathway** that most other
  countries embed into their contracts. What we have is a
  **temporary‑staff model** that keeps us in a constant state of limbo,
  with **no guarantee of continuation, promotion, or even adequate
  compensation**.  That’s why, even knowing that other nations use
  contracts, I still feel the need to look for a place where the
  contract **means something** – where it is a **bridge to permanence**,
  not a **dead‑end trap**.  If Malaysia wants to retain its doctors, we
  need to **re‑design the contract**:  1. **Link each contract to a
  training/career pathway** with clear milestones.   2. **Index
  salaries** to inflation and provide a realistic on‑call allowance.
  3. **Introduce statutory redundancy/renewal protections** and a
  transparent appeals process.   4. **Empower a collective bargaining
  body** that can negotiate contract terms on behalf of doctors.  Until
  those changes happen, the contract‑only reality will continue to push
  many of us, myself included, to seek a more secure and rewarding
  professional future abroad.    — Dr Lim Kah Wai, Medical Officer,
  Malaysia.
