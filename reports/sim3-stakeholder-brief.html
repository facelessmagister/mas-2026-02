Six Key Findings

Evidence from the multi-agent simulation across 8 rounds and 14 stakeholder types.

01
Measured Intake Expansion
Graduates rise from 4,820 to 6,500, but each increase is gated on verified post creation. No 2016-style glut — intake and posts grow in parallel.
02
Post-Graduate Matching
Housemanship posts grow from 3,500 to ~6,000, eliminating the bottleneck that produced the original contract-doctor crisis.
03
Contract System Phased Out
Permanent posts expand +8%/year. Contract doctors drop from 15,600 to 5,000. Career certainty is restored — the #1 retention driver.
04
Retention Improves Steadily
Brain drain falls from 1,200 to ~800/year. Returnees rise from 95 to 200/year. Career pathways become predictable again.
05
Working Conditions Ease
Average workload falls from 72 to ~58 hours/week. Overcrowding eases from 76 to 58. Both approach sustainable thresholds.
06
Student Interest Recovers
After initial reform uncertainty, interest rebounds from 1.75 to 2.06 as medicine regains reputation as a secure career.

Conclusion

Summary judgment for stakeholders and decision-makers.

Measured Expansion Works

Intake rising to 6,500 is safe ONLY when matched to post creation. The 2016 glut is avoided.

Contract Abolition is Critical

Permanent posts expanding +8%/year is the single biggest retention driver. Contract doctors drop 68%.

Retention Metrics Improve

Brain drain −30%, returnees +111%, retention +12pp. The workforce stabilizes without heroic measures.

Trajectory to 2038

At this pace, Malaysia reaches 3.0 doctors/1,000 by 2038 — the WHO threshold for universal health coverage.

Risk Assessment

This requires sustained political will. A change of government could freeze post creation while intake continues, recreating the structural mismatch. The next simulation (SIM-4) tests whether fixing retention and working conditions alone — without increasing intake — can also resolve the shortage.
